Adding References in Kryptopedia

Why References Are Important

References are essential for establishing credibility and reliability in wiki content. They allow readers to:

  • Verify the information provided
  • Explore topics in more depth
  • Assess the quality of the research behind an article
  • Follow the development of ideas and concepts

All factual claims in Kryptopedia should be backed by reliable references whenever possible, especially when presenting:

  • Technical information
  • Statistical data
  • Quotes or statements attributed to specific individuals or groups
  • Controversial information
  • Cutting-edge or emerging knowledge
Tip: Good references enhance the credibility of your contributions and help build a trusted knowledge base for all users.

Reference Formatting

Basic Reference Syntax

Kryptopedia uses a simple reference system for citations. You can add references using the following syntax:

[^1]: Author, A. (Year). Title of the work. Publisher. URL

To cite this reference in the text, use:

This is a statement that needs a citation[^1].

The footnote references will appear at the bottom of the article in a "References" section.

Example:

Bitcoin was created by an individual or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto[^1].

References:

[^1]: Nakamoto, S. (2008). Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System. https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf

Important: Make sure to place the reference definitions at the end of your article, ideally under a "References" heading.

Citation Styles

Kryptopedia supports several common citation styles, including:

Style Example Common Use
APA Smith, J. (2020). Title of article. Journal Name, 10(2), 30-45. Technology, Psychology, Social Sciences
MLA Smith, John. "Title of Article." Journal Name, vol. 10, no. 2, 2020, pp. 30-45. Humanities, Arts, Literature
Chicago Smith, John. "Title of Article." Journal Name 10, no. 2 (2020): 30-45. History, Arts, Business

Choose a citation style that's appropriate for your topic and be consistent throughout your article.

Sources for URLs and DOIs

For online references, include URLs or DOIs (Digital Object Identifiers) whenever possible:

[^1]: Smith, J. (2020). Title of the article. Journal Name. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx

DOIs are preferred over URLs when available, as they are permanent identifiers that won't change even if the website address changes.

Reliable Sources

What Makes a Source Reliable?

When adding references to Kryptopedia, prioritize sources that are:

  • Accurate: Factually correct and precise
  • Verifiable: Other users can check the source
  • Notable: Recognized within the relevant field
  • Published: Properly published rather than self-published
  • Recent: Up-to-date, especially for technical topics
  • Peer-reviewed: Academic sources that have undergone review
Good sources include:
  • Academic journals and conference proceedings
  • Books from reputable publishers
  • Industry standards and technical documentation
  • Reputable news sources and specialized publications
  • Official documentation and white papers
  • Government and institutional reports
Exercise caution with:
  • Personal blogs or social media posts
  • Forums and discussion boards
  • Self-published materials without editorial oversight
  • Sources with clear commercial biases
  • Articles without named authors or publication dates

Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Sources

Different types of sources have different values in research:

  • Primary sources: Original documents, data, or accounts (e.g., research papers, patents, white papers)
  • Secondary sources: Analyses or interpretations of primary sources (e.g., review articles, textbooks)
  • Tertiary sources: Collections or summaries of secondary sources (e.g., encyclopedias, bibliographies)

When possible, prefer primary and reliable secondary sources over tertiary sources.

Advanced Reference Techniques

Reusing the Same Reference

To cite the same reference multiple times, you can use the same reference identifier:

Bitcoin was proposed in 2008[^1]. The system went live in January 2009[^1]. [^1]: Nakamoto, S. (2008). Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System. https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf

Adding Notes to References

You can add additional notes or context to your references:

[^1]: Nakamoto, S. (2008). Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System. https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf. This is the original white paper that introduced Bitcoin to the world.

Organizing Multiple References

When an article has many references, organize them under a clear "References" heading at the end of the document:

## References [^1]: First reference [^2]: Second reference [^3]: Third reference
Tip: Keep your reference numbers sequential to make it easier for readers to follow along. Consider using a reference management tool if you're working with many sources.

Common Issues and Solutions

Handling Missing Information

Sometimes you may not have all the information for a perfect citation. Here's how to handle common issues:

Missing Information How to Handle
No author Use the organization name or "Anonymous" if appropriate
No date Use "n.d." (no date)
No page numbers Omit page numbers or use paragraph numbers if appropriate
Website no longer available Try to find an archived version using the Internet Archive (archive.org)

Troubleshooting References

Common issues with references and how to fix them:

  • References not appearing: Make sure the reference definition format is correct and placed at the end of the article
  • Reference numbers not linking: Check that the reference ID in the text matches exactly with the reference definition
  • Formatting problems: Ensure there's a space between the text and the reference marker
